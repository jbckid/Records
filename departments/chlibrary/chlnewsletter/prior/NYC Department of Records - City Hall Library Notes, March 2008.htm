SPOTLIGHT ON: WOMEN'S HISTORY MONTH

By Christine Bruzzese

Since 1978, the U.S. Congress has designated March as Women's History Month. The City Hall Library contains a diverse collection of resources on women who have played significant roles in New York City politics, history and culture.

My Fight for Birth Control was written by Margaret Sanger and published in 1931. Working as a nurse on the Lower East Side, Sanger observed the plight of women suffering physically and mentally because of unwanted pregnancies. She established the first contraceptive clinic in New York and despite arrest and prosecution, continued to advocate for contraceptive freedom. Sanger's autobiography describes her pioneering work and her life. She was born in 1879 and died in 1966.

Women-Owned Businesses in New York City: Results of the First Survey of a Growing Economic Force was published in 2001. This study was prepared by the New York City Comptroller's Women's Economic Development Task Force and the New York Women's Agenda Economic Development Committee. Women business owners in New York City were surveyed about pertinent issues. Recommendations are given for developing government programs to support women-owned businesses.

Abby Aldrich Rockefeller by Mary Ellen Chase tells the story of the matriarch of the Rockefeller family and patron of the arts. Abby Aldrich was born in 1874 and married John D. Rockefeller, Jr. in 1901. As well as being the mother of Governor Nelson D. Rockefeller, Abby Aldrich Rockefeller was instrumental in establishing the Museum of Modern Art and supporting American artists and artisans. She died in 1948.

Publications of the New York City Commission on the Status of Women may also be of interest to the researcher. This agency is now known as the New York City Commission on Women's Issues.

Additionally, vertical and biographical files provide a wealth of information on the unique contributions of women to New York City life.
